# Research Basis

Use this file to justify the default U.S. equity signal priorities. Do not claim that any technical method has a universal highest win rate. Academic evidence is mixed, varies by sample period and costs, and is vulnerable to data-snooping. Treat the ranking below as a prior that must be recalibrated on the user's actual symbol, timeframe, market regime, and data.

## Evidence-Prior Ranking for U.S. Equities

1. Trend-following with moving averages and range breakouts.
   - Brock, Lakonishok, and LeBaron (1992) tested moving average and trading range breakout rules on the Dow Jones Index from 1897 to 1986 and found buy signals tended to have higher subsequent returns than sell signals, with bootstrap tests rejecting several null models.
   - Han, Yang, and Zhou (2011/2013 versions) found moving-average timing applied to volatility-sorted portfolios often outperformed buy-and-hold, especially in high-volatility portfolios, even after transaction costs in their study.

2. Momentum / relative strength over intermediate horizons.
   - Jegadeesh and Titman (1993) documented that buying recent winners and selling recent losers generated positive returns over 3- to 12-month holding periods.
   - Use this as stronger evidence for mid-term and long-term U.S. equity analysis than for intraday analysis.

3. Volume-confirmed momentum and breakout.
   - Lee and Swaminathan (1998) found past trading volume helps predict the magnitude and persistence of future price momentum. Volume adds information about investor interest and reversal risk.
   - Give volume higher weight when confirming breakouts and momentum, lower weight when isolated.

4. Pattern recognition and multi-signal confirmation.
   - Lo, Mamaysky, and Wang (2000) proposed systematic technical pattern recognition and found several technical indicators contained incremental information in U.S. stocks from 1962 to 1996.
   - Use chart patterns and divergence as supporting evidence, not as sole decisive signals.

5. MACD, RSI, and oscillator combinations.
   - Evidence for standalone MACD/RSI rules is weaker and more sample-dependent. A U.S. stock-market MACD study on arXiv found standalone MACD win rate below 50% in its tested sample, while combining MACD with momentum indicators such as RSI/MFI improved win rate.
   - Use MACD, RSI, and KDJ as confirmation, timing, exhaustion, and divergence tools rather than dominant predictors.

## Required Cautions

- Sullivan, Timmermann, and White (1998/1999) emphasize data-snooping bias and bootstrap reality checks for technical trading rules. Always penalize rules discovered only by parameter search without out-of-sample validation.
- Marshall, Sun, and Young (2007) found popular rules were rarely profitable across U.S. stocks during 1990-2004, with some evidence of better results in smaller, less liquid stocks. Do not overstate broad U.S. stock applicability.
- Use transaction costs, slippage, shorting constraints, turnover, and tax horizon when interpreting "win rate" or probability.
